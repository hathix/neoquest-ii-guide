introduction

Ello there. If you were looking for that elusive Fountain of Youth, I think you made a wrong turn at Albuquerque. If you were looking for sunflower seeds, diabolical Chias, and amnesiac Techos, I think you've found your page. Congratulations.

So what is this page about? Surely not diabolical Chias, right? Well, no, not really. This is a NeoQuest II guide. If you take the time to read this page, you'll chat with wizards with attitude, eat a big ugly dragon for breakfast, and maybe even get the girl! OK, maybe not that last one, but as for the rest, satisfaction guaranteed.

The burning question is: why did the chicken cross the road what is NQII? In short, NQII is like sunflower seeds: it may not look that attractive, but it's addicting if you try it. Trouble is, both are tough nuts to crack (groan.) You can consider this guide the nutcracker. We'll break through the tough outside to get to the tasty beating-up-obese-stuff inside. Mmm.

So, uh, who am I? Good question. I have many names, including "crazy" and "Knucklehead McSpazatron", but you can just call me Rev. My goal here is to make NQII more synonymous with "diabolical Chias" than "boring.

Will Rev ever become sane? Will this introduction ever end? Will you ever get to beat up diabolical Chias? Read on to find out...

beginner's guide

You must be the new kid in town. Welcome. Well, you won't be very welcomed in this city if you don't know a thing about NQII (believe me, I've been there before.) Good thing you're reading this guide. In these next paragraphs, I'll be giving you a crash course on all things NQII.

First things first: in NQII you control four Neopets: Rohane the arrogant Blumaroo, Mipsy the violent Acara, Talinia the (comparatively boring) Eyrie, and Velm the amnesiac Techo. As you travel through the game, these guys will join you and help you in your quest to beat up baddies. *triumphant fanfare*

Enough of that. Let's get to the actual game. Basically, you want to gain experience from bashing baddies (thereby making you gain a level) so that you're more prepared to beat up the bosses (which is a fancy term for uglier, more overweight baddies.) After each battle, the experience points that the baddies drop is given to your characters. If a character gains enough experience, they level up, gaining more max HP (Hit Points, Health Points, or Happy Pigs; take your pick) and earning a skill point. For now we'll leave it at that.

Seems like everyone from those annoying Lupes to diabolical Chias is loaded. In addition to giving EXP, baddies drop gold for you. How convenient. This cash can be used to buy stuff (we'll go over "stuff" later.) and stay at inns. We'll talk about that more in the Playing NeoQuest II Section.

Oh yeah, and monsters also drop healing potions (dude, these guys have big pockets.) Repeat after me: we'll go into more detail later. Good, good.

Dude, I came here to learn how to actually, you know, play the game. Not learn about the contents of baddies' pockets. OK, OK, relax. I'll tell you that there are 5 acts, or chapters which you play through. While you're doing this, you'll want to gain levels (max level is 60), buy stylish new armor/weapons, beat up a bunch of fat guys, and - in the end - lol at a huge ugly dragon and, maybe, get the girl! (Maybe I lied about that last one. *shifty eyes*)

Congrats, you've just leveled up from noob to novice. A few more sections and you'll be a right pro.

playing neoquest ii

Nice of you to join us. Grab a cup of coffee/tea/soda/motor oil (for the robotically inclined) as you learn a bit more about NQII. There's a lot here so feel free to skim (not skip, skim.)

  • Difficulty Modes: after you beat Normal mode (that's the default one), you can play Evil mode. Once you beat Evil you can play Insane. The modes get tougher, but the prizes get way better as you win harder modes. (As an added treat, monsters can sometimes drop weapons/armor in Evil and Insane.) Here's what you can expect in each mode:
    • Normal: Nothing much to say here.
    • Evil: Monsters have 50% more HP than on Normal, bosses have 25% more HP, stuff costs 30% more, and enemies drop less gold. It's not as hard as it sounds.
    • Insane: Monsters have double the HP than on Normal, bosses have 50% more HP, stuff costs 60% more, and enemies drop even less gold. Also, if all your characters die you have to start all over again. Muhahaha.

  • The Overworld: yeah I know it has a funny name, but this is the screen you'll be seeing a lot when you're playing. The Overworld shows Rohane in an ego-centric position (he's always in the - surprise surprise - center of your screen.) The overworld can be seen up there ^. From the overworld, you can walk (and chew gum), talk to peeps, look at your inventory, or... that's about it, actually. Speaking of which, here's a pic of your inventory screen.
    PIC HERE
    Click on a character to see their skills (more on that later.) If you look at the blue table thingy, you'll see all the potions/weapons/random junk that's sitting in your backpack. From here, you can use potions, equip weapons, and scrutinize random junk. If that so pleases you.
  • Walking: who knew that walking could be so hard? To tell the truth, it isn't. Just click the arrows in the compass rose which corresponds in the way you want to go. If you want to go west, hit left. Brilliant eh? Also, there are two modes of travel: Normal (wonder what that could be...) and Hunting (where you have a better chance of being attacked by baddies.)
  • Towns: baddies need a place to terrorize, and towns are the best things for doing so. Well, from your point of view towns are a welcome place to buy/sell stuff. Most towns have an inn (where you can pay to heal yourself up all the way), potion merchant, and weapon/armor merchant. When you talk to a merchant, you can either buy from his inventory (the table on top) or sell from yours (the table on bottom.) And remember: townspeople keep their cities small (one square small) to hid themselves. Funny thing is, the minute you step on a town you're teleported inside a bustling metropolis with a full 10 people living there (if you're lucky.)
  • Potions: in the original NQ, there were only Healing Potions. Not to be outdone, NQII has five.
    • Healing Potions: just as they sound. They can be used in or out of battle.
    • Damage Potions: no, these don't let you show your emo side. Instead, you toss these at enemies (normally bosses) to get some damage against them.
    • Haste Potions: these potions speed up the guy who used them. Not too commonly used.
    • Slow Potions: toss one at a boss and watch him move in slow-mo. Just remember that the slow wears off after a while, so re-throw potions if needed.
    • Revival Potions: if someone has fainted/passed out/died, use one of these guys on him/her and make them ready to beat up baddies again.
  • Monsters: Remember, Johnny, monsters don't exist. Johnny's been lied to. Baddies are everywhere in NQII, just begging to get squished/mauled/whatever. In fact, they approach you. When you encounter one (or two, three, or four) you'll be taken to a battle screen. Here's one for you.
    PIC HERE
    When fighting monsters, click on their picture so they turn into a living target. Then attack (Rohane and Talinia can just hit the red button, but Mipsy needs to choose a spell in blue) until it dies. If there is more than 1 enemy, kill one at a time. Check your guys' health (and time till next action) at the bottom of the screen, and heal if necessary (if your health bar is yellow.) Have everyone else ignore him and carry on their business. Rohane should use a healing potion on himself when it's his turn. Once that's done, keep punching. If you've noticed, there's a Next Turn value under everyone's picture. In the weird world of NQII, time flows straight (whoa!) but only moves if you click a button. If Rohane's next turn is in 2 seconds and a diabolical Chia's in in 3, Rohane will get his turn before the Chia does. Whoever has the smallest time till Next Turn moves next. Once the monster has been beaten, you gain EXP, Gold, and maybe even some potions.
  • Bosses: hate your boss? Take your anger out on these overweight dudes with no fashion sense. Bosses are conveniently placed at the end of some dungeons and at the end of each act. Whenever you face a boss, your first instinct should be to throw slowing potions until one works. After that, haste your party or set up shields (if possible) and then start attacking with melee, spells, or damage potions. Watch your health and re-apply potions as needed. Once the boss dies, you get EXP, NP, gold, and usually weapons/armor. Always equip the dropped weapons/armor. Make sure you sell the old weapons/armor when you're back in town.
  • Leveling Up: when you gain enough EXP to fill up the yellow bar thingy, you level up. As you might expect, your level goes up by one and your EXP bar is reset. As you might not expect, you are completely healed and your max HP goes up a bit. You also gain a skill point to spend wherever you like.
  • Skills: since you level up 59 times during the game, you gain 59 skill points. Each of your guys has 7 skills, and you can spend a max of 15 points in each skill. As the skill level goes up, the usefulness of the skill goes up too. See the skills guide a little below you to see where to spend these funky skill point thingies.
  • Dungeons: call em what you like: caves, buildings, temples. They're all the same: make your way up or down until you meet a boss. Beat the boss and then take the (probable) teleporter behind his carcass to exit the dungeon.
  • Maps: if you plan to follow the Walkthrough, I highly recommend maps. Just use your search engine of choice and search for NeoQuest II Maps. Bookmark that site and keep it open as you go through the game.

skills guide

Wanna get to the walkthrough right now? If so you can skip this section... although you should read it later. Eager beaver. At any rate, I'll tell you the skill name, description, and my thoughts for each skill. If you notice, I sometimes tell you to put points somewhere at the end. Don't be scared, this is because you should stop spending skill points around level 53. Once you get your final weapons (I'll let you know when), you can distribute those points. You'll see why when you get to that point.

  • rohane

    Rohane is your standard sword-and-shielf warrior, sans shield. He can take plenty of hits with his high HP, and he can deal lots of damage as well. He's kinda cool, actually.

    • Critical Hits (CH): (The official name is Critical Attacks, but no one uses that.) Anyhow, you know how Rohane normally punches people in the face? With this skill, he can sometimes hit their stomachs to inflict (normally 20% to 50%) more pain and suffering. Keyword: sometimes.
      ~Great skill to have, especially later in the game when Rohane can deal 150+ damage on a CH. Rohane's CHs are something to be scared of (if you're a baddie of course.)
    • Damage Increase (DI): Don't be fooled. This skill actually reduces Rohane's risk of spontaneously combusting. Some say that it increases his average damage output. Lolz.
      ~More damage = more fun. This skill is really useful when Rohane is by himself or with just Mipsy, since Rohane will be carrying a lot of weight. Definitely get this.
    • Combat Focus (CF): Instead of being a haughty Ima-beat-you-up dude, Rohane decides to become a haughty Ima-not-listen-to-you fellow. He does less damage, but also takes less.
      ~Cool skill (and really annoying when baddies use it), but Rohane's meant to be an offensive force. Besides, he can already take plenty of hits. Not that useful.
    • Stunning Strikes (SS): I may beat up on Rohane sometimes (read: a lot), but he's still pretty cool. With this, he can sometimes shock the other guy with his coolness and set back their turn a few seconds.
      ~I like this skill, since if opponents can't hit you they can't hurt you. It tends to be resisted on harder difficulties and later bosses, but it's still worth spending a few points in.
    • Battle Taunt (BT): You know how you feel like punching someone when they make Yo Mama jokes? With this skill, Rohane makes a ton of them, encouraging baddies to focus their attacks on him.
      ~Fun skill, but you don't need to invest heavily to get the most effect out of it. A few points are always useful, and if you have extra skill points at the end of the game you can put them here. Oh yeah, and yo mama climbed over a glass wall to see what was on the other side.
    • Innate Magic Resistance (IMR): When baddies use magic on Rohane, he just turns his head and takes less damage or resists the attack completely. Hence the name Magic Resistance.
      ~This skill definitely helps with boss battles, but it's not absolutely necessary until Act 4, where everyone and their mother uses magic. If you have spare skill points at the end of the game, put some here.
    • Innate Melee Haste (IMH): When you invest here, Rohane invests in 4-Hour-Energy to make himself go faster. After he attacks, he has less reload time before he can attack again.
      ~Rohane hits really hard, and with this skill he can hit fast as well. Hitting hard AND fast? I like it. Put plenty of points here.

  • mipsy

    Mipsy is a wizard with attitude. She lol'z at stuff with her mighty spells, but she takes hits about as well as a wet paper towel. But still, a wizard with attitude? That's pretty sweet.

    • Direct Damage (DD): The DD vs. GDD debate is right up there with the butter vs. jelly debate. DD deals up to 100 damage to a single opponent and is pretty fast.
      ~I (and most others) prefer this one over its cousin GDD. Most people kill baddies one by one because it's faster and safer that way (you have less things attacking you.) Early on, DD lets Mipsy kill stuff in 2-3 hits, and later it's a nice consistent chunk of damage.
    • Group Direct Damage (GDD): DD's little cousin. GDD deals up to 64 damage to each opponent but is a good bit slower.
      ~Some people like this skill, but it's so slow that you might as well use DD (which is roughly twice as fast.) Not to mention it's not as good against bosses. But hey, it's neat skill.
    • Group Haste (GH): Mipsy conjures 4-Hour-Energy and gives some to everyone on your team. Amazingly, everyone moves a bit faster.
      ~Mipsy can't do much outside of mauling stuff... but this skill is extremely useful against bosses. If you slow them and speed yourself, you'll be doubly fast and have a much easier time. Put plenty of points here, but only after other skills have been built up.
    • Slowing (Slow): This spell lets Mipsy, inexplicably, slow down the opponent without having to use slow potions.
      ~Ironically, the fact that it does the same thing as slow potions does it in. Why spend all those points here when you could just toss potions? And besides, Mipsy has better stuff to be doing (i.e. zapping stuff in the face.)
    • Damage Shields (DS): Mipsy uses the Force to make a shield that hurts things that try to attack you melee. Baddies who use magic get through unscathed.
      ~This skill is cool, but not worth spending major points on. At the end of the game, any spare points you get can go here. Chip damage does add up, after all.
    • Innate Melee Defense (IMD): Remember how Mipsy takes hits like a wet paper towel? Imagine drying out the towel and making it 5 times as thick. That's what this skill does to Mipsy's defense.
      ~Even with plenty of points here, Mipsy isn't a brick wall by any means. This skill is awesome, though, because it actually allows Mipsy to take a few hits before dying. Which is always cool.
    • Innate Casting Haste (ICH): Mipsy grabs her 4-Hour-Energy and uses it to speed herself up. Just as you'd expect.
      ~You might be noticing a theme: Haste is awesome. With points here, Mipsy gets more chances to spam DD. Fun fun fun.

  • talinia

    Talinia's your archer, and she's pretty similar to Rohane in that she can take some damage and deal it out too. What sets her apart are her awesome collection of goodies (aka skills), and just for that she's awesome.

    • Increased Bow Damage (IBD): Wonder what this one could do. Perhaps it allows Talinia to eat tofu faster. Hmm...
      ~Why not invest here? Talinia doesn't hit that hard by default, so pump plenty of points in here to make her a macho-Eyrie.
    • Multiple Targets (MT): When Talinia feels like it, she can fire a bunch of arrows to hit, well, multiple targets. With 1 point invested, she fires 2 arrows; with 6, 3 arrows; and with 11, 4 arrows. The more points you spend, the less time it takes between uses.
      ~This skill is really cool for softening up the enemy party. It's like GDD but a whole lot better, TBH. I like putting 10 points in here: she can fire 3 arrows every 10-15 seconds by Act 5. Meanwhile, at 11 points she fires 4 arrows every 20-25 seconds. Why fire 4 arrows once a battle when you can fire 3 arrows twice?
    • Ranged Attacks (RA): Tally practices her sniping (and antisocial) skills by fighting from farther away than everyone else. If someone tries to hit her (melee or magic), their next turn is set back a bit.
      ~Neat skill. Monsters are more likely to hit characters who hit them first. Thanks to Talinia's MT, she'll be sticking out like a sore thumb. And thanks to RA, baddies are effectively stunned each time they try to hit Tally. Plus it's hilarious seeing a boss stunned for 10 seconds when he uses GDD.
    • Shockwave (SW): Not just an Internet plugin. No, this guy works like Rohane's SS, except it stuns everything when it works. Keyword: when it works.
      ~Looks cool on paper, but RA outclasses it IMO. RA lands a guaranteed stun every time, while Shockwave isn't nearly as reliable. Still, if you have spare points at the end you can put 'em here.
    • Slowing Strike (SS): Somehow, Talinia manages to slow baddies a few percent each time she hits them. Then again, baddies carry gold in their pockets, so anything's possible in NQII.
      ~Eh. The effects of this are minimal (only 5% slow at max level) and wear off pretty quickly. Put your points elsewhere.
    • Innate Magic Resistance (IMR): Talinia doesn't like diabolical Chias throwing spells at her, so she's gotten this skill. When you spend points here, she's more resistant to magic. *gasp*
      ~Similar to Rohane. Put a few points here in the beginning and some more toward the end (if you're wondering, you encounter diabolical Chias in late Act 4.)
    • Innate Melee Haste (IMH): Wonder what this one could do. Perhaps it allows Talinia to eat tofu faster. Hmm...
      ~You can probably guess what I'm going to say here. The more points you put here, the more times Tally will be able to hit those diabolical Chias.

  • velm

    Unless you've never looked at this page's header or background (which would be pretty hard), you'll know that Velm (the gang's healer) is my favorite NQII character. He's got amnesia and the game's best pickup line: "Don't fidget. And get a haircut!

    • Healing (Heal): Remember DD vs. GDD? Well, it's back in the form of Heal vs. Group Heal. Scandalicious. Healing, uh, heals any one character for up to 150HP, and it's faster than GH.
      ~Unfortunately for the tabloids, there isn't *that* much debate over Heal vs. GH. Most people (including me) go with GH, which is more efficient and convenient. You'll see why in a moment.
    • Group Healing (GH): Muhahaha. Velm makes healing potions as useful as wisdom teeth thanks to this skill. Although it's slower than Healing, GH heals all your guys and gals for up to 90HP.
      ~You see why we use GH over Healing? Most of the time all your guys are banged up a little. With Healing you'd have to heal them individually. With GH, all you do is click a button and everything's all peachy. NQII'ers are lazy so we love this skill.
    • Group Shielding (GS): Velm too can use the Force. Unlike Mipsy, he makes a shield that makes your team take a little less damage from all attacks.
      ~Algebra time (o noez!): GS + GH = most of your defense problems solved. You may not notice its effects when you use it, but when baddies start using GS later in the game you'll notice the difference. What more can I say? Use it.
    • Mesmerization (Mes): Has Velm ever put you in a trance with his coolness? Probably not, but he can still put baddies in a trance, preventing them from doing anything. Baddies snap out of it if they're hit or healed.
      ~Fun skill. Problem is, it gets resisted a lot on harder difficulties and later acts. You don't need too many points here for it to work, so spend a few points here (duh) and maybe put some here at the end of the game if you have spare points.
    • Celestial Hammer (CH): See, Mipsy is horribly scared of cockroaches (they're immune to her nukes you see), so she convinced Velm to invest in a hammer. At max level, he can deal 45 damage and a 3-second stun to the hapless roaches (and other assorted baddies.)
      ~Telling Velm to go on the offensive is like telling Payton Manning to play cricket: they're not made for it. Velm has better things to be doing than killing roaches, namely, healing the team. Besides, Velm's melee attack does almost as much as this (kinda sad.)
    • Innate Melee Defense (IMD): By investing here, Velm ups his coolness to such levels that baddies just can't hit him as hard.
      ~Dead Velm = Sad you. So yeah, invest here.
    • Innate Casting Haste (ICH): Velm grabs some Blue Bull (he prefers it over 4-Hour-Energy) and uses it to go faster (duh.)
      ~GH and GS both take a while to reload, so Blue Bull is vital for our amnesiac friend.

trestin village

Good luck! I've packed you a sword and a barrel. What's the barrel for? The monkeys, of course!

Well, this is where it all begins (and, for plenty of peeps, where it all ends.) Good thing you're not one of those peeps. And since you're not one to quit, switch to Hunting mode and head west to exit town. Don't know how to? Click here to learn how to adventure. You'll wake up back in Trestin. Leave town by going west, and start snacking on Plains Lupes (if you're hurt, talk to Mother to heal.) New to battling? Click here to learn how to battle. Don't go too far until you've gotten to level 2/3/4.

Now you can start fighting in the hills to your north. Gain a level here, then head west until all the mountains are to your east. Then go north until you hit a forest. Start killing monsters until you're level 4/5/6, using healing potions if needed. No idea how to? Click here to learn about your inventory.

(in)frequently asked questions

Here, you can find the answers to all the questions submitted by you made up by me. Although if you have a question feel free to contact me. I don't bite. Much.

  • Help! I've gotten lost in a cave and can't get out!
    First off, that isn't a question. *glares* Second, here's what you want to do. Pretend you're Rohane. Now walk over to a wall and put your left (or right) hand on it. Start walking forward, but do it so that your hand stays on that wall. Eventually, you'll come across a boss or exit.
  • What's with the level 10/11/12 stuff?
    Thanks for asking a question for once. To avoid compromising our information to our enemies, I write in code. 10/11/12 means that you should be level 10 if on Normal, 11 if on Evil, and 12 on Insane.
  • The monsters here are too tough! What can I do?
    Your best bet is to backtrack a bit (i.e. exit a cave, leave a desert), which should make the baddies you fight easier.
